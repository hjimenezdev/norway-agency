Welcome to Norway

Norway, nestled in the breathtaking landscapes of Scandinavia, is a country that captivates with its stunning natural beauty and rich cultural heritage. Renowned for its fjords, Norway boasts a coastline adorned with dramatic cliffs and serene, deep-blue inlets that carve through the rugged terrain. The iconic Geirangerfjord and Nærøyfjord, both UNESCO World Heritage Sites, showcase the country's unique blend of towering mountains, cascading waterfalls, and pristine waters.


Beyond its awe-inspiring landscapes, Norway embraces a deep cultural tapestry. The capital city, Oslo, is a vibrant hub of modernity and tradition, boasting world-class museums such as the Munch Museum and the Viking Ship Museum. Norway's rich maritime history is evident in its coastal towns like Bergen, where colorful wooden houses line the waterfront and the iconic fish market provides a sensory delight.


Norway's commitment to sustainability and environmental conservation is exemplary, with a focus on renewable energy and eco-friendly practices. The nation's social policies, including universal healthcare and free education, contribute to a high standard of living. In essence, Norway beckons visitors with its harmonious blend of natural wonders, cultural treasures, and a commitment to preserving the beauty that defines this Nordic gem.

History

Viking Age (8th-11th centuries)

In the early history of Norway, the Viking Age marked a crucial period. Vikings, skilled navigators and warriors, explored and colonized vast regions, from the shores of the North Atlantic to the Caspian Sea. Norway, with its intricate coastline, was a vital center for these raids. The Norwegian sagas, literary epics recounting heroic deeds, capture the essence of this era, highlighting figures like Olaf Tryggvason and Harald Hardrada.

Unification and Christianization (1000 - 1300)

In the 11th century, Norway experienced unification under the reign of Harald Hardrada. This period also witnessed the Christianization of the country, with Olaf II (St. Olaf) playing a crucial role in introducing Christianity as the official religion. The construction of churches and the adoption of Christian traditions transformed Norwegian society and integrated it more closely into medieval Europe.

Union with Denmark and Sweden (1300 - 1800)

Norway entered into a union with Denmark in the 14th century, followed by a union with Sweden in the 17th century. This period was marked by the loss of autonomy and the influence of these neighboring nations on Norwegian affairs. The Black Plague also had a devastating impact on the population and economy of the country.

Independence and Constitution (1814)

After centuries of Danish rule, Norway experienced a brief period of independence in 1814. However, this was followed by a forced union with Sweden. Despite the union, Norway managed to retain its own constitution and legal system, laying the foundations for its future development as a sovereign nation.

Resistance during World War II (1940-1945)

During World War II, Norway was invaded by German forces in 1940. Norwegian resistance, supported by the Allies, played a crucial role in the country's liberation. Internal resistance and the evacuation of the royal family to London symbolized the determination of the Norwegian people in the face of Nazi occupation

Economic and Social Development (2000 -Present)

After the war, Norway experienced remarkable economic development, largely driven by the oil industry discovered in the 1960s. The country became one of the most prosperous in the world, with a robust welfare system and active participation in international affairs. Norway has maintained its unique cultural identity while adapting to the challenges and opportunities of the 21st century.

Most popular destinations

Oslo

Oslo, the capital of Norway, is a captivating blend of modernity and history set against a backdrop of natural beauty. Nestled between the Oslofjord and green hills, the city boasts iconic landmarks like the Royal Palace and Akershus Fortress. With a commitment to sustainability, Oslo is known for its well-preserved parks, efficient public transportation, and a vibrant cultural scene, including museums like the Viking Ship Museum.

Bergen

Bergen, situated on Norway's southwestern coast, captivates visitors with its enchanting blend of historic charm and natural splendor. Framed by seven hills and overlooking the iconic UNESCO-listed Bryggen Wharf, the city boasts a unique architectural character characterized by colorful Hanseatic buildings. Rich in maritime history, Bergen's bustling fish market and the Hanseatic Museum offer glimpses into its seafaring past.

Read more

Ålesund

Ålesund, a picturesque town on the western coast of Norway, is renowned for its unique architecture , stunning coastal setting, and rich maritime heritage. The town's distinctive art nouveau buildings, a result of its reconstruction after a devastating fire in 1904, create a charming and characterful atmosphere. Situated amidst a cluster of islands, Ålesund offers breathtaking views of the Norwegian Sea and surrounding fjords.

Most popular natural parks

Jotunheimen-Beitostølen

Jotunheimen National Park and the nearby town of Beitostølen together offer a captivating blend of pristine natural beauty and outdoor adventure in Norway. Jotunheimen, translated as "Home of the Giants”, is the country's largest national park, featuring awe-inspiring landscapes of towering peaks, glaciers, and valleys. Popular mountaineering, the park is home to Northern Europe's highest peak, Galdhøpiggen.

Read more

Jostedalsbreen

Jostedalsbreen National Park, located in western Norway, is a mesmerizing expanse of natural wonders, dominated by the largest glacier in continental Europe, the Jostedalsbreen Glacier. This national park encompasses a diverse landscape of dramatic ice formations, deep valleys, and snow-capped peaks. The glacier itself is a breathtaking sight, with its icy tongues extending into various arms, creating a stunning glacial panorama.

Read more

Folgefonna

Folgefonna National Park, situated in southwestern Norway, is a captivating haven of glacial landscapes and natural beauty. The park is renowned for hosting the Folgefonna Glacier, the third-largest glacier in Norway. The glacier's expansive ice fields, crevasses, and icefalls create a mesmerizing spectacle against the backdrop of surrounding mountains and deep fjords. Visitors to Folgefonna can explore the glacier's accessible arms.
